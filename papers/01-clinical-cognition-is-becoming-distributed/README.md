# Clinical Cognition Is Becoming Distributed

Positioning: field-defining position paper for the Clinical Cognition Transformation Lab (CCTL).

## Abstract

Clinical cognition has traditionally been studied as an activity centered in the individual clinician: a physician, dentist, nurse, or other health professional gathers evidence, reasons through uncertainty, forms judgments, and acts. That framing remains indispensable, but it is becoming incomplete. In contemporary healthcare, clinical cognition increasingly emerges across distributed systems that include clinicians, AI systems, documentation artifacts, institutional workflows, healthcare organizations, patients, and caregivers.

This paper argues that the central scientific question is no longer only how clinicians reason, or whether AI systems perform well in isolation. The emerging question is how clinical cognition transforms when intelligence becomes distributed across human and artificial participants. AI does not merely automate tasks at the edge of clinical judgment. In many settings, it changes what is noticed, what is summarized, what is remembered, what is delegated, what is documented, and what later actors can reconstruct. CCTL proposes Human-AI Clinical Cognition as a research program for studying these transformations with conceptual, empirical, and methodological rigor.

## Introduction

Healthcare is entering a period in which clinical judgment is increasingly mediated by computational systems. AI tools summarize records, generate differential diagnoses, recommend next steps, draft notes, interpret images, rank risks, translate clinician language for patients, and shape the sequence of clinical work. These systems are often evaluated as tools: useful or harmful, accurate or inaccurate, efficient or inefficient.

Tool evaluation is necessary, but it does not fully capture the cognitive changes now underway. When AI systems participate in documentation, triage, recommendation, explanation, or interpretation, they may alter the cognitive structure of clinical work itself. They may redistribute attention, compress uncertainty, substitute mechanisms, produce new frames, or shift the work of explanation from one actor to another.

This paper proposes that clinical cognition is becoming distributed. The claim is not that individual clinicians no longer matter. Rather, the claim is that the clinically meaningful unit of analysis is expanding. Clinical cognition increasingly resides in relationships among people, systems, records, workflows, institutions, and patients.

## From Individual Reasoning to Distributed Clinical Cognition

Clinical reasoning has often been analyzed through the mind of the clinician: hypothesis generation, pattern recognition, analytic reasoning, uncertainty management, and decision-making under constraint. This tradition has produced essential insights and should not be abandoned.

However, many clinically important cognitive events now occur outside the boundary of any one clinician's reasoning process. A referral letter may compress a complex diagnostic concern into a brief phrase. A specialist note may reconstruct an upstream rationale that was never fully documented. A treatment plan may convert probabilistic judgment into operational sequencing. A patient portal message may translate clinical uncertainty into practical action. An AI-generated summary may introduce a diagnosis, omit a caveat, or reorder the salience of prior evidence.

These are not peripheral communication events. They are cognitive transformations. Clinical meaning, uncertainty, responsibility, and intent move through artifacts and actors. Each transition can preserve, distort, simplify, enrich, or reassign cognition.

Distributed clinical cognition therefore requires a broader analytic frame. The object of study is not only what an individual clinician thought, but how cognition is produced, transformed, and acted upon across a system.

## Why AI Changes the Unit of Analysis

AI changes the unit of analysis because it increasingly participates in the production of clinical interpretation. A model that detects risk, summarizes a chart, drafts a diagnosis, generates patient-facing language, or recommends a plan is not merely accelerating an existing workflow. It can alter the workflow's cognitive content.

This does not mean AI has professional judgment, moral agency, or clinical accountability in the way humans do. Those distinctions remain essential. But it does mean AI can shape the cognitive pathway that leads to a clinical decision. It can determine which facts become prominent, which uncertainties are visible, which options are considered, and which explanations are preserved for downstream review.

If AI is studied only as a tool, the analysis tends to focus on performance against a task. If AI is studied as a participant in a distributed cognitive system, the analysis can ask a different set of questions: What changed in the reasoning pathway? What was delegated? What was compressed? What became harder to audit? What new form of expertise became necessary for human oversight?

The shift is subtle but important. The question is not only whether AI helps clinicians think better. The question is how thinking itself changes when clinical cognition is shared across humans, machines, and artifacts.

## Human-AI Clinical Cognition

Human-AI Clinical Cognition refers to the distributed cognitive processes that emerge when clinical decisions are jointly produced by clinicians, AI systems, documentation artifacts, institutional workflows, healthcare organizations, patients, and caregivers.

This concept treats cognition as system-level activity. A clinician may still hold the final responsibility for interpretation or action, but the path to that action may include AI-generated summaries, prior documentation, institutional protocols, handoff artifacts, patient preferences, and organizational constraints. The final decision may be human, but the cognitive pathway may be distributed.

Human-AI Clinical Cognition therefore invites study of cognition as movement. It asks how clinical claims, uncertainties, recommendations, explanations, and responsibilities change as they move from one participant or artifact to another. It also asks how clinicians adapt when some forms of cognitive labor are amplified, assisted, delegated, or displaced by AI.

## Forms of Clinical Cognition Transformation

Clinical cognition may transform in multiple ways as it moves through distributed human-AI systems.

- Preservation: relevant reasoning, uncertainty, and intent remain traceable across transitions.
- Compression: complex reasoning is reduced to a conclusion, label, recommendation, or operational instruction.
- Delegation: a cognitive task such as summarization, image interpretation, risk ranking, or language generation is assigned to AI or another actor.
- Translation: specialist reasoning is converted into patient-facing language, administrative language, or cross-disciplinary communication.
- Simplification: complexity is reduced to support workflow execution, consent, scheduling, or adherence.
- Reframing: a later actor or AI system changes the organizing problem or diagnostic frame.
- Reconstruction: downstream reviewers infer prior cognition from incomplete artifacts.
- Redistribution: cognitive labor moves among clinicians, AI systems, staff, patients, caregivers, and institutions.
- Reassignment: responsibility for noticing, deciding, explaining, or acting shifts across roles.
- Drift: recommendations, diagnoses, or treatment intent gradually change across repeated transitions.

These transformations are not inherently good or bad. Some are necessary for care. Others may create risk. The scientific task is to observe them, characterize them, and understand their consequences.

## Implications for Medical Education

If clinical cognition is becoming distributed, medical education must prepare clinicians not only to reason individually but also to supervise, interrogate, and participate in human-AI cognitive systems.

Future expertise may include the ability to recognize when AI has compressed uncertainty, when a summary has changed the diagnostic frame, when a recommendation has substituted a mechanism, or when a downstream artifact has lost the rationale needed for accountability. Clinicians may need stronger metacognitive skills: not only asking, "What do I think?" but also, "How was this cognitive pathway produced?"

This does not imply that foundational clinical knowledge becomes obsolete. On the contrary, robust domain knowledge may become more important when clinicians must detect subtle errors, omissions, or distortions in AI-mediated work. The educational question is not whether clinicians should learn less because AI can assist them. It is how clinicians should learn differently when clinical cognition is distributed.

## Implications for Documentation and Accountability

Documentation has always been more than record keeping. In distributed human-AI systems, documentation becomes a cognitive interface: it preserves, compresses, translates, and sometimes transforms the reasoning that later actors inherit.

This raises accountability questions. If an AI-generated summary changes the salience of evidence, if a note omits uncertainty, or if a treatment plan converts a tentative hypothesis into a firm operational pathway, who can reconstruct what happened? Which parts of cognition remain auditable? Which assumptions were introduced by humans, by AI systems, by templates, or by institutional workflow?

Traditional documentation practices may not be sufficient for AI-mediated clinical cognition. CCTL therefore treats cognitive provenance as a core research problem: the study of how clinical claims, evidence, uncertainty, recommendations, and responsibilities can be represented and traced across transitions.

## Research Agenda

CCTL frames Clinical Cognition Transformation as a research program rather than a product or software standard. Its initial agenda includes five related programs.

First, Clinical Cognition Transformation studies how cognition changes across healthcare systems, including diagnostic reframing, recommendation drift, uncertainty compression, cognitive delegation, and patient-facing simplification.

Second, Cognitive Reconstruction asks whether prior cognition can be reconstructed from downstream artifacts such as referral letters, consultation notes, treatment plans, clinical documentation, and longitudinal records.

Third, Human-AI Co-Cognition studies hybrid cognitive systems in which humans and AI jointly produce clinical interpretation, recommendation, explanation, or action.

Fourth, Cognitive Provenance studies how cognitive pathways can be represented, traced, audited, and understood.

Fifth, Longitudinal Cognitive Change studies how clinician cognition evolves as AI becomes embedded in daily practice: which abilities are amplified, which are delegated, which weaken, and which new forms of expertise emerge.

Together, these programs shift attention from isolated AI performance to the transformation of clinical cognition within distributed systems.

## Conclusion

Clinical cognition is no longer exclusively an individual cognitive activity. It is increasingly a distributed process emerging from interactions among clinicians, AI systems, documentation artifacts, institutions, and patients.

This shift does not diminish the role of clinicians. It makes their role more complex. Clinicians remain essential interpreters, supervisors, explainers, and accountable actors. But the cognitive environment in which they work is changing.

The scientific challenge is no longer understanding clinical reasoning alone. The scientific challenge is understanding how clinical cognition transforms within distributed human-AI systems. CCTL exists to study that transformation: cautiously, empirically, and with attention to the future of clinical expertise, documentation, accountability, and patient care.
